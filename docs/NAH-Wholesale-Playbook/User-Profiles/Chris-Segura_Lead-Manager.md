## Overview

Chris Segura is the relationship builder on the NAH lead management team. Where Daniel drives volume, Chris drives connection. His natural warmth and conversational style allow him to build rapport quickly with sellers, particularly those in emotional or distressed situations. Chris has a talent for getting sellers to open up about their real motivations — divorce, foreclosure, financial hardship, health issues — which is the foundation of effective lead qualification. His challenge is channeling that natural rapport into a more structured, disciplined call framework.

## What Chris Does Well

**Rapport and Emotional Connection.** Chris's greatest strength is his ability to connect with sellers on a human level. His coaching feedback consistently highlights phrases like "excellent empathy and active listening" and "effectively extracted the true motivation." In one notable call, a seller going through a divorce opened up completely: "We're just getting ready to go through a divorce and we're looking to sell it as it is... we both are very committed that the house needs to sell." Chris created the space for that disclosure through genuine warmth and patience.

**Motivation Extraction.** When Chris is engaged in a substantive conversation, he excels at uncovering the real reason a seller wants to sell. He draws out foreclosure deadlines, financial distress details, and emotional motivations that are critical for the AM to use during the closing process. One call showed him uncovering a seller facing foreclosure with a 6-day deadline and owing $95,000 — exactly the kind of deep qualification that drives deals.

**Handling Emotional Sellers.** Chris naturally gravitates toward the Affirmer persona from Jerry Norton's framework. When sellers are grieving, stressed, or overwhelmed, Chris meets them where they are. His response to a 70-year-old widow selling her late husband's cabin — "I'm sorry to hear that" followed by patient listening — exemplifies the empathetic approach that builds trust with distressed sellers.

**Appointment Setting with Context.** When Chris schedules an appointment for Kyle, he provides context and sets expectations: "His name is going to be Kyle. I'm going to put Kyle for this one." He also confirms specific times and offers text reminders, which reduces no-show rates.

**Adaptability.** Chris shows strong adaptability when calls take unexpected turns. In one top-scoring call, a seller immediately raised an issue about a missing contract instead of discussing the expected topic. Chris pivoted smoothly: "Oh, really? Let me check here." This ability to handle surprises without losing composure is a valuable trait.

## Where Chris Needs Development

**Filler Words and Verbal Tics.** The most frequently cited improvement across Chris's coaching feedback is his excessive use of "Okay" as a filler. One coaching note documented a string of over 50 consecutive "Okay"s in a single response. While acknowledging the seller is important, varying affirmations ("Got it," "I understand," "That makes sense") would make conversations tighter and more professional.

**Call Structure and Expectation Setting.** Chris tends to jump into conversations organically rather than following a structured opening. His coaching feedback consistently recommends adding the standard expectation-setting framework: "Is it okay if I share how we work? I'll ask questions about your property and situation. If not a fit, let's say so. If yes, I'll schedule with our franchise owner to present an offer. Fair?" This structure reduces seller resistance and gives Chris a framework to fall back on.

**Price Anchoring.** When sellers state their asking price, Chris sometimes moves past it without anchoring expectations for a cash offer. His coaching notes repeatedly suggest using the price anchoring technique: "I see other investors buying properties at 40–50% of comparable value. That's not our offer — just what the market looks like for cash. How does that land with you?" This technique would help manage expectations before the AM call.

**Accuracy Under Pressure.** In at least one documented call, Chris misstated a seller's asking price ($140,000 became $40,000 in his follow-up question). While this may have been a slip, accuracy with numbers is critical in real estate transactions. Slowing down to confirm numbers verbally would prevent this.

**Decision-Maker Identification.** Chris occasionally misses the step of asking whether anyone else has input on the selling decision. The coaching principle is simple: always ask "Is there anyone else who would need to be part of this decision?" Failing to identify a spouse, family member, or co-owner early creates cancellation risk later.

**Professional Language.** One low-scoring call flagged the use of "Hey honey" as a greeting in a professional sales call. While Chris's warmth is an asset, maintaining professional language boundaries is important for credibility.

## Communication Style

Chris's natural communication style is **warm, conversational, and empathetic**. He builds trust through personal connection and genuine interest in the seller's situation. He is at his best in longer conversations where he has time to develop rapport and draw out motivation.

His style maps most closely to the **Amiable/Expressive** personality types — relationship-focused, emotionally attuned, and patient. This is a significant strength when dealing with Expressive and Amiable sellers, but he should practice being more direct and efficient when dealing with Driver-type sellers who want to get to the point quickly.

Chris's natural empathy is a rare and valuable skill in sales. The coaching focus should be on adding structure and discipline around that natural talent, not replacing it.
