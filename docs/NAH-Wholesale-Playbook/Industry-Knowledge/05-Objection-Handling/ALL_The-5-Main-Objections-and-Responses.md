# The 5 Main Objections and How to Handle Them

**Applicable To:** All Roles
**Source:** Todd Toback (No Limits REI) + Steve Trang (Disruptors/OPS) — Negotiation, Objection Handling, Frameworks

---

## Overview

Every objection a seller raises falls into one of five categories. Mastering the response to each one covers 95% of real-world scenarios. The key principle: an objection is not a rejection. It is a request for more information, reassurance, or a different framing.

---

## Objection 1: "I Want More Money"

This is the most common objection and the easiest to mishandle. The instinct is to justify your number or raise your offer. Both are wrong.

**Response — Go back to pain:**
"What were you hoping to get?"

Let them state their number. Then explore the gap between their number and yours by revisiting their motivation. The price objection is almost never about price — it's about whether the seller feels the trade-off is worth it.

**Response — Reframe the value proposition:**
"If speed and certainty matter more than top dollar, this might make sense. If getting the absolute highest price is the priority, listing with an agent might be the better path."

This is Truth Telling (Todd's technique). By honestly presenting the alternative, you force the seller to weigh what they actually value. Most motivated sellers value speed and certainty over an extra $10K that might take 6 months to materialize.

**Mindset shift for the closer:**
> "Double your target assignment fee. The size of your deal is what you expect."

Don't negotiate against yourself. If you expect $5K assignments, you'll get $5K assignments. If you expect $20K, you'll structure deals differently and hold firmer.

---

## Objection 2: "I Need More Time"

This is the most dangerous objection. "More time" kills more deals than any other response because it feels reasonable. The seller isn't saying no — they're saying "not yet." But "not yet" often becomes "never."

**Response — Timeline Compression:**
"How long has this been going on? What's your drop-dead date?"

Establish how long the problem has existed. If they've been dealing with it for 2 years, another month won't change anything. Make the cost of waiting concrete.

**Response — Get specific:**
"Would 30 days be fair? If we could close in 30 days and you could move on, would that work?"

Vague timelines ("a few weeks," "sometime soon") are deal killers. Pin down a specific date and get agreement.

**Response — Condition for pushback:**
"What if they say no? How do we handle that?"

If the seller says they need to "talk to their spouse" or "think about it," pre-handle the potential negative outcome. "If your spouse says no, what would you want to do?" This prevents the conversation from ending in limbo.

**Response — Future pace:**
"If this is handled, are we ready to move forward Monday?"

Project past the objection. If the seller agrees to a future commitment contingent on their concern being resolved, you've essentially closed — you just need to help them resolve the concern.

---

## Objection 3: "I Want to Shop Around / Get More Offers"

This objection means the seller doesn't yet see enough differentiation in your offer to commit.

**Response:**
"That's smart. What are you looking for that other offers aren't providing?"

This is Reversing (Todd's technique). Instead of defending your offer, find out what the seller actually wants. The answer reveals the gap — and it's usually not price. It's speed, certainty, flexibility on closing date, or willingness to buy as-is.

Once you identify the gap, you can position your offer as the one that fills it.

---

## Objection 4: "I Don't Know Your Company"

This is a trust objection disguised as a credibility question.

**Response — Reverse:**
"Why is that important to you? What would make you feel comfortable?"

Find out what specifically concerns them. Is it fear of being scammed? Worry about the process? Uncertainty about whether you'll actually close? Each concern has a different remedy.

**Response — Match their concern with relevant proof:**
- If they're worried about legitimacy → show reviews, references, proof of past closings
- If they're worried about the process → walk them through exactly what happens step by step
- If they're worried about closing → offer an earnest money deposit or proof of funds

---

## Objection 5: "I Need to Think About It"

This is the vaguest objection and almost always masks a specific concern the seller hasn't voiced.

**Response:**
"What specifically do you need to think about?"

This question forces the seller to articulate their real concern. "I need to think about it" is not an objection — it's a container for the real objection. Your job is to open the container.

Common real objections hiding behind "I need to think about it":
- "The price is too low but I don't want to negotiate"
- "My spouse won't agree"
- "I'm not sure I trust you"
- "I'm not ready to let go of the property emotionally"

Once the real objection surfaces, you can address it directly using the appropriate technique.

---

## The Meta-Principle

> "All sales is tied to emotion, not logic."

Every objection has a logical surface and an emotional core. The logical surface is what the seller says ("I want more money"). The emotional core is what the seller feels ("I'm afraid I'm being taken advantage of"). Address the emotion, and the logic resolves itself.
