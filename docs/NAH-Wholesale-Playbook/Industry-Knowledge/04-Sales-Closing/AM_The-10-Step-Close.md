# The 10-Step Close

**Applicable To:** Acquisition Manager
**Source:** Todd Toback (No Limits REI) — Rapport, Negotiation, 10-Step Sales System

---

## Overview

The 10-Step Close is a structured framework for moving a qualified seller from initial conversation to signed contract. The critical insight is that the offer comes LAST — not first. Most wholesalers lead with price and lose. This system builds commitment before price is ever discussed.

> "Rapport > Price — we were $40K LOWER and still won."

## The 10 Steps

### Step 1: The Pour

Build rapport intentionally. This is not small talk — it is strategic relationship building. Find common ground, show genuine interest in the seller's situation, and establish yourself as a human being, not a faceless investor.

The pour sets the emotional foundation for everything that follows. A seller who likes you will accept a lower offer. A seller who doesn't trust you will reject a higher one.

### Step 2: Set Agenda and Ask Permission

Tell the seller exactly how the conversation will go. Then ask permission to proceed.

Example: "Here's what I'd like to do — I'll ask you some questions about the property and your situation, and then if it makes sense for both of us, I'll put together a fair offer. Sound good?"

This does two things: it gives the seller a sense of control, and it creates implicit agreement to participate in the full process.

### Steps 3–4: Qualify

Two questions must be answered before moving forward:

1. **Do they have a PROBLEM?** — Not "do they want to sell" but "is there a real problem that selling would solve?" Divorce, foreclosure, inheritance burden, tired landlord, relocation, tax liens — these are problems. "Curious what it's worth" is not a problem.

2. **Will they trade equity to solve it?** — A motivated seller with a problem but unwilling to accept a discount is not a deal. The seller must understand that speed, certainty, and convenience come at a cost.

### Step 5: Review Commitments

Before handling objections or making an offer, review what the seller has already committed to:

- "What happens next?"
- "Yes or no?"
- "When do we talk?"

This step locks in the seller's own words. They've told you their problem, their timeline, and their willingness to act. Now you're holding them to their own commitments, not yours.

### Steps 6–8: Handle Objections

Objections are not rejections — they are requests for more information or reassurance. Use the 5 Communication Techniques (see below) to address each objection without becoming defensive or aggressive.

The key principle: objections handled BEFORE the offer is made are 10x easier to resolve than objections raised AFTER the offer.

### Step 9: The Offer

The offer comes LAST. By this point, the seller has:
- Built rapport with you (Step 1)
- Agreed to the process (Step 2)
- Confirmed they have a problem (Steps 3–4)
- Made commitments about next steps (Step 5)
- Had their concerns addressed (Steps 6–8)

The offer is now a natural conclusion, not a cold pitch. The seller is psychologically prepared to say yes because they've been saying yes throughout the entire conversation.

### Step 10: The Boomerang

After the seller agrees, pull back slightly:

"Are you sure? Is this a good fit for you?"

This seems counterintuitive but serves a critical purpose. The seller re-affirms their emotional commitment to the deal. This prevents cancellations — when the seller's sister calls the next day and says "you could have gotten more," the seller has already re-affirmed their decision and is less likely to back out.

> "The worst thing is 'Let's do it!' Because later he thinks 'I could have got more.' Then his sister calls."

## The 5 Communication Techniques

These techniques are used throughout the 10 steps, particularly during objection handling:

### 1. Cushioning

Soften every response before delivering it. Remove pressure from the conversation.

Example: "If it's not a fit, that's okay. I just want to make sure we explore whether this makes sense for you."

### 2. Reversing

Answer questions with questions. This keeps the seller talking and reveals their true concerns.

Seller: "What's your offer?"
You: "Before I get to that — what were you hoping to get?"

### 3. Truth Telling

Be blunt. Say the thing the seller doesn't expect to hear from a buyer.

Example: "Honestly, you'd probably net more listing with an agent. Maybe that's the better path for you."

This triggers a paradoxical response — the seller starts defending why they DON'T want to list with an agent, revealing their real motivation.

### 4. Stealth Mismatch

Give the opposite of what the seller expects. When they expect you to push, pull back. When they expect you to agree, challenge.

This disrupts the seller's mental script and brings them back toward you. People resist pressure but pursue what seems to be moving away from them.

### 5. Getting Commitments

Define the next step in painstaking detail. Not "I'll call you next week" but "I'll call you Tuesday at 2pm. If I don't reach you, I'll try again Wednesday morning. If we can't connect by Thursday, what should I do?"

Vague next steps die. Specific commitments survive.

## The Friction Principle (Post-Signing)

After the contract is signed, do NOT celebrate. Slow it down. Add friction.

> "Don't celebrate. The worst thing is excitement. Because later he thinks 'I could have got more.'"

A calm, professional close — "Great, I'll get this processed and my team will be in touch about next steps" — creates less buyer's remorse than high-fiving and saying "Awesome, we got a deal!"

> "All sales is tied to emotion, not logic."
