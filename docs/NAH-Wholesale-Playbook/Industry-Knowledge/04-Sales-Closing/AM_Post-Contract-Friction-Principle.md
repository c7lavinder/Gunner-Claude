# The Post-Contract Friction Principle

**Applicable To:** Acquisition Manager
**Source:** Todd Toback (No Limits REI) — Negotiation, Contract Retention, Cancellation Prevention

---

## The Problem

The most dangerous moment in a wholesale deal is not the negotiation. It is the 24–72 hours after the seller signs the contract.

> "The worst thing is 'Let's do it!' Because later he thinks 'I could have got more.' Then his sister calls."

After signing, the seller enters a period of doubt. They second-guess the price. They call a family member who tells them they're being taken advantage of. They Google "am I getting scammed by a cash buyer." They get cold feet.

This is where deals die — not at the negotiation table, but in the seller's living room the next morning.

---

## The Friction Principle

When the seller says yes, do not celebrate. Do not show excitement. Do not rush to get the paperwork signed. Instead, slow down and create deliberate friction.

**The counterintuitive approach:**

1. **Pause after the yes.** Let silence sit for a moment. This signals that you're thoughtful, not desperate.

2. **Express mild hesitation.** "Before we move forward, I want to make sure this is really the right decision for you. Can I ask you a couple more questions?"

3. **Review the downsides.** "Just so we're clear — you're giving up the chance to list this on the market and potentially get more. You're okay with that?"

4. **Let them re-sell themselves.** When you present the downsides, the seller will argue back with their own reasons for selling. These self-generated reasons are far more powerful than anything you could say.

5. **Confirm commitment slowly.** "If you wake up tomorrow and your brother calls and says 'why did you do that,' what are you going to tell him?"

---

## Why It Works

The friction principle works because it addresses the cancellation risk before it happens. By forcing the seller to confront their doubts during the call — while you're still present to guide the conversation — you prevent those doubts from festering overnight into a cancellation.

When a seller has already articulated their reasons for selling, defended their decision against hypothetical objections, and confirmed their commitment in specific terms, they are psychologically anchored to the deal. The sister who calls the next day gets met with: "I already thought about that. Here's why I'm doing this."

---

## The Boomerang Close

This is Step 10 of Todd's 10-Step Close, and it directly implements the friction principle.

After the seller agrees to the deal:

"Are you sure? Is this really a good fit for you?"

This question seems like it could lose the deal. In practice, it does the opposite. The seller responds with their own reasons:

"Yes, I'm sure. I've been dealing with this property for two years. The taxes are eating me alive. I just want it gone."

Now the seller has re-affirmed their emotional commitment. They've said it out loud. They own the decision. This is dramatically harder to walk back than a simple "yes" followed by your celebration.

---

## Post-Signing Protocol

After the contract is signed, the friction principle continues:

1. **Same-day follow-up call.** Within 2–4 hours of signing, call the seller. Not to discuss the deal — to check in as a person. "Hey, just wanted to make sure you're feeling good about everything. Any questions?"

2. **Next-day text.** A simple message: "Hi [name], just checking in. We're moving forward on our end. Let me know if you need anything."

3. **Proactive updates.** Don't wait for the seller to wonder what's happening. Send updates on title work, inspection scheduling, and closing timeline. Silence breeds doubt.

4. **Anticipate the interference.** If the seller mentions a family member, friend, or advisor who might weigh in, address it proactively: "When you talk to your daughter about this, she might have some questions. Would it help if I spoke with her directly?"

---

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Dangerous | What to Do Instead |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Celebrating after the yes | Signals the seller that you "won" and they "lost" | Stay calm, move to the Boomerang |
| Rushing to sign paperwork | Creates pressure that triggers buyer's remorse | Slow down, review terms carefully |
| Going silent after signing | Seller fills the silence with doubt | Follow up same day and next day |
| Ignoring the "sister factor" | Third-party interference is the #1 cancellation cause | Pre-handle it during the call |
| Skipping the Boomerang | Seller never re-affirms their own decision | Always ask "Are you sure?" |

---

## Grading Implications

Calls where the closer rushes past the commitment or shows visible excitement at the yes should be flagged. The friction principle is a discipline — it requires the closer to suppress their natural reaction and prioritize deal retention over momentary satisfaction.

The best closers treat the yes as the beginning of the retention process, not the end of the sales process.
