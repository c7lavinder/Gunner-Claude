# Qualification Questions for Closers

**Applicable To:** Acquisition Manager
**Source:** Todd Toback (No Limits REI) + Chandler Saine (Level Up REI) — Qualification, Discovery Process

---

## The 5 Core Qualification Questions

These questions form the backbone of every seller conversation. They are designed to uncover motivation, timeline, competition, and willingness to act — in that order.

### 1. "What's got you thinking about selling?"

This is the most important question in the entire conversation. The answer reveals the seller's motivation — the emotional driver behind the decision. Listen for:

- **Strong motivation:** Divorce, foreclosure, inheritance burden, relocation, tired landlord, tax liens, code violations, health issues
- **Weak motivation:** "Just curious what it's worth," "Thinking about it someday," "Exploring options"

Strong motivation means there is a problem that selling solves. Weak motivation means there is no urgency and the deal will likely stall.

### 2. "What happens if you don't sell?"

This question reveals the cost of inaction. If the seller says "nothing, I'll just keep it," there is no urgency. If the seller says "I'll lose the house to foreclosure" or "I'll keep bleeding $2,000 a month," there is a real deadline driving the decision.

The cost of inaction is your strongest negotiating tool. When the seller articulates it themselves, they are selling themselves on acting now.

### 3. "What's your ideal timeline?"

Timeline separates hot leads from warm ones. A seller who says "as soon as possible" is hot. A seller who says "sometime in the next year" is warm at best.

Get specific. "As soon as possible" means different things to different people. Pin it down: "If we could close in 3 weeks, would that work?"

### 4. "Have you spoken with anyone else?"

This question reveals competition and expectations. If the seller has received other offers, you need to know:
- How many offers?
- What were they?
- Why didn't they accept?

The answer to "why didn't they accept?" is gold. It tells you exactly what the seller values that the other offers didn't provide. Match that, and you win.

### 5. "If we could work something out that's fair, would you be open to meeting?"

This is the commitment question. It's not asking for a "yes" to a specific number — it's asking for a "yes" to the process. A seller who agrees to meet has psychologically committed to exploring a deal. The meeting itself creates momentum.

> "Diagnose before prescribing."

## The Sorting Mindset

The purpose of qualification is to sort, not to convince.

> "Professionals sort, amateurs convince."

A professional wholesaler uses these questions to quickly determine whether a deal exists. If the seller has no motivation, no timeline, and no willingness to act, the professional moves on. An amateur spends 45 minutes trying to create motivation that doesn't exist.

Time spent convincing one unmotivated seller is time not spent finding three motivated ones. The math always favors sorting.

> "80% of 'unmotivated' sellers ARE motivated."

The caveat: many sellers who initially seem unmotivated are actually motivated but guarded. The qualification questions, asked with genuine curiosity and without pressure, often unlock motivation that a direct pitch would never reveal. The key is asking the questions and listening — not pushing.
