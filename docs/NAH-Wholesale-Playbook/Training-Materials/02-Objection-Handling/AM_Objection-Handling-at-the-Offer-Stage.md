# Objection Handling at the Offer Stage

**Role:** Acquisition Manager
**Category:** Objection Handling

> Common objections sellers raise after hearing the offer and how to acknowledge, reframe, and guide the conversation.

---

# 🛡️ Objection Handling at the Offer Stage — Acquisition Managers

> Objections at the offer stage are **good.** They mean the seller is engaged enough to push back. Your job is to **acknowledge, reframe, and guide** — not argue.

---

## 🎯 The Framework: Acknowledge → Reframe → Question

Every objection gets the same three-step treatment:

1. **Acknowledge** — Show you heard them and it's a reasonable reaction
2. **Reframe** — Shift perspective to what they're actually getting
3. **Question** — Ask something that moves the conversation forward

---

## 🗣️ The Objections

### 1. "That's too low."

> **You:** "I hear you — and I respect that. Can I ask, what number were you hoping to see?"
>
> **Seller:** "[Their number]"
>
> **You:** "Okay, let me walk you through how we got to ours, and then maybe we can find some middle ground."

---

### 2. "My neighbor's house sold for way more."

> **You:** "That's a great reference point. Was their house in similar condition, or had they done some updates? … The difference is we're buying yours exactly as it sits. When you factor in the repairs we're taking on, that's where the gap comes from."

---

### 3. "Let me think about it."

> **You:** "Absolutely, take your time. Just so I can follow up properly — what specifically do you want to think over? Is it the number, the timing, or something else? … And when would be a good time for me to check back in?"

⚠️ **Never let "let me think about it" end without a callback time.**

---

### 4. "I need to talk to my spouse."

> **You:** "Of course — that makes total sense. Would it help if I put together a quick summary you can share with them? And how about I call back [day] to see where you both land?"

---

### 5. "I can get more listing it."

> **You:** "You might be right. But here's what that looks like:
>
> - **$[X] in repairs** just to get show-ready
> - **6% in commissions** — that's $[X]
> - **Closing costs:** another $[X]
> - **3-6 months waiting** = $[X] more in carrying costs
>
> When you net all that out, our number is pretty close to what you'd walk away with — and you'd have the money in **two weeks**, not six months."

---

### 6. "Another investor offered me more."

> **You:** "That's great — you should absolutely consider it. Can I ask, is their offer also as-is, or are there contingencies? … A lot of offers come with inspection periods, financing contingencies, or renegotiation clauses. Ours is cash, no contingencies, close when you want. Sometimes the higher number on paper doesn't end up being the higher number in your pocket."

---

### 7. "I owe more than that on the mortgage."

> **You:** "I appreciate you sharing that. How far behind are things? … There may be some options we can look at, like working with your lender or structuring this differently. Let me talk to my team and see what we can figure out."

---

### 8. "Zillow says it's worth $X."

> **You:** "Zillow's a good starting point, but their estimate assumes the house is in retail-ready condition. They don't know about the [roof/HVAC/foundation]. Once you factor in repairs to get it to that Zillow number, the actual as-is value is quite a bit different."

---

### 9. "I'm not in a rush."

> **You:** "That's fine — no pressure. But the carrying costs keep going — taxes, insurance, maintenance. Sometimes waiting costs more than people realize. Our offer is good now, and it takes the burden off."

---

### 10. "I want to get an appraisal first."

> **You:** "Totally your right. Just keep in mind, an appraisal values it as if a retail buyer is purchasing with a loan — not on an as-is, cash basis. The two numbers will be very different, and that's normal. If it'd give you peace of mind, go for it."

---

### 11. "Can you come up on the price?"

> **You:** "Help me understand what number would make this work for you. … Let me see if there's any room on our end. I can't promise anything, but I want to see if we can make this work for both of us."

---

### 12. "I don't trust investors."

> **You:** "I totally get that — there are some bad actors out there. We've been buying homes in Nashville for years. I'm happy to share references or connect you with homeowners we've worked with. Everything's in writing, you'd have your own closing attorney, and you're never locked into anything you're not comfortable with."

---

### 13. "What if I just rent it out?"

> **You:** "That's an option. Have you been a landlord before? … With the condition the property is in, you'd need $[X] in repairs before renting, plus you're dealing with tenants, maintenance, and vacancy risk. Our offer lets you cash out now without any of that."

---

### 14. "My friend/family said I should get more."

> **You:** "It's smart to get input from people you trust. Most people's idea of home value is based on retail sales of move-in-ready homes. Your property as-is for cash with no fees — that's a different market. Happy to walk through the numbers with them on the phone if that'd help."

---

## ⚡ Rules of Engagement

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| **Never get defensive** | Their objection isn't personal |
| **Never argue** | You'll win the argument and lose the deal |
| **Always end with a question** | Keeps the conversation moving forward |
| **Silence is your weapon** | After responding, stop talking and let them process |
| **Know when to walk away** | Integrity means they might call you back later |

---

## 📋 Quick Reference

1. **Acknowledge → Reframe → Question** — every objection, every time
2. **"That's too low" is good** — it means they're negotiating, not walking away
3. **Reframe around total cost of alternatives** — repairs, commissions, time, carrying costs
4. **Never let "let me think about it" end without a callback time**
5. **Know when to walk away honestly** — integrity builds long-term deals
