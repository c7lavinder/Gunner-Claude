# Counter-Offer Strategy for Acquisition Managers

**Role:** Acquisition Manager
**Category:** Objection Handling

> How to handle seller counter-offers — when to hold, when to bump, and how to negotiate without losing the deal.

---

# 🔢 Counter-Offer Strategy for Acquisition Managers

> If a seller counters, it means they **want to sell** — they just want more money. That's a negotiation, not a rejection.

---

## 📊 Know Your Numbers First

Before any counter-offer conversation, know these **cold:**

| Number | What It Is |
|---|---|
| **ARV** | After Repair Value — what the house is worth fully renovated |
| **Repair Estimate** | Cost to get it there |
| **Holding Costs** | Taxes, insurance, utilities, payments during rehab (3-6 months) |
| **Closing Costs** | Both sides — buying and selling |
| **Profit Margin** | What makes this deal worth doing |
| **MAO** | Maximum Allowable Offer = ARV - Repairs - Holding - Closing - Profit |

🚨 **If you don't know your MAO, you can't negotiate.** You'll either overpay or walk away from a good deal.

---

## ✋ When to Hold Firm

### Hold when:

- You're already at or near your MAO
- The repairs are significant and well-documented
- The seller is motivated and has few alternatives
- You've clearly explained how you got to your number

### How to hold:

> **You:** "I appreciate you coming back with that number. I've run the numbers pretty carefully, and with the repairs — the roof, the HVAC, the foundation work — $[your offer] is really where we need to be. I wish I could go higher, but I'd rather be honest than overpromise."

### The walkaway hold:

> **You:** "I don't want to waste your time. If $[your offer] doesn't work, I completely understand. Explore your options. But if you circle back and that number works down the road, give me a call. The offer stands."

💡 **Walking away with grace often brings sellers back within days or weeks.**

---

## 📈 When to Move Up

### Move when:

- You have room between current offer and MAO
- The seller is reasonable and close to your range
- A small bump ($2K–$5K) could close the deal
- The property has upside you haven't fully accounted for
- Deal velocity matters (fast close = lower holding costs)

### How to move:

> **You:** "I hear you. Let me see what I can do. … I can come up to $[new number], but that's really my ceiling on this one. At that price, we're taking on a lot of risk with the repairs. Can we shake on that?"

### ⚠️ Rules for Moving

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| **Never jump to your MAO** | Move in small increments |
| **Get something in return** | "I can come up if we can close by [date]" or "if you leave the appliances" |
| **Never move more than once without them moving too** | It's a negotiation, not an auction against yourself |
| **Present movement as difficult** | "Let me talk to my partner" — makes the concession feel real |

---

## 🔧 Using Repair Costs to Support Your Position

This is your **strongest tool.** The seller can see the damage.

> **You:** "I know $[offer] feels low compared to what houses are selling for around here. But let me show you what we're looking at:
>
> | Repair | Estimated Cost |
> |---|---|
> | Roof | $12,000 |
> | HVAC | $7,000 |
> | Foundation | $15,000 |
> | Kitchen update | $12,000 |
> | Flooring throughout | $8,000 |
> | Paint, trim, fixtures | $5,000 |
> | **Total** | **$59,000** |
>
> Houses in this neighborhood sell for around $220K fully renovated. Back out the repairs, holding costs, and closing costs on both sides — $[offer] is where the math lands."

💡 **Transparency builds trust.** When sellers see actual numbers, the offer makes sense.

---

## 📉 Using ARV to Support Your Position

> **You:** "I pulled comps for renovated homes in your area. The three most recent sales were $215K, $225K, and $218K — so about $220K as the target. But that's AFTER we put in $60K in renovations. That's the gap between retail and as-is."

---

## 🎯 Handling Specific Counter Scenarios

### Seller counters $10K–$20K above your offer

Small gap. Often closeable.

> **You:** "We're not that far apart. What if we split the difference at $[midpoint]?"

---

### Seller counters $30K+ above your offer

Big gap. Need to educate.

> **You:** "I understand where you're coming from. Let me walk through the repair breakdown again — I think that's where the disconnect is."

---

### Seller won't budge at all

> **You:** "I respect that. Let me leave my offer on the table — it's good for [timeframe]. If things change or you want to revisit, just call me."

---

### Seller says "That's my final number"

> **You:** "I appreciate the honesty. Let me re-run everything one more time and see if there's any way I can make your number work. I'll call you back by [time]. But if the numbers don't work, I'll tell you straight."

---

## ⚡ The Golden Rules

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Know your MAO before you start** | Never negotiate blind |
| **Never negotiate against yourself** | Wait for their counter. Don't bid yourself up. |
| **Every concession should feel hard-won** | Don't give room away easily |
| **Get something for something** | Faster close, leave items, sign this week |
| **Be willing to walk away** | The deal you can't leave is the deal you overpay on |

---

## 📋 Quick Reference

1. **Know your MAO cold** — ARV - Repairs - Holding - Closing - Profit = your ceiling
2. **Hold firm when you're at your number** — be honest, not apologetic
3. **Move in small increments and get something back** — never bid against yourself
4. **Use repair breakdowns** — transparency makes your number make sense
5. **Walking away with grace brings sellers back** — leave the door open
