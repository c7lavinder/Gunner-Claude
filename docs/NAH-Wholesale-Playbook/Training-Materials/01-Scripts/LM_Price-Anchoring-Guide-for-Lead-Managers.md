# ⚓ Price Anchoring Guide for Lead Managers

> The first number mentioned in any negotiation becomes the **anchor** that everything else is compared to. If the seller anchors high, you're fighting uphill. If you anchor first, you set the frame.

---

## 🧠 The Psychology

When someone hears a number — any number — their brain latches onto it. Everything after gets measured against that first number.

- Seller says **"I want $200K"** first → your $130K offer feels like an insult
- You anchor at **$120K–$140K range** first → your $135K offer feels reasonable

This isn't manipulation. It's helping sellers understand what **as-is cash offers actually look like** in the real world.

---

## ✅ When to Anchor

| Anchor When… | Don't Anchor When… |
|---|---|
| You've **extracted motivation** and understand their situation | **Too early** — no rapport or motivation yet |
| They ask **"what can you offer?"** | You have **no property info** — can't anchor blind |
| You've **built rapport** and they're engaged | The seller is **emotional/upset** — read the room |
| You can get your number out **before they give theirs** | They've already **firmly stated their price** |

---

## 🎯 How to Anchor: The Framework

### Step 1: Set the Context

Explain **WHY** cash offers are different from retail:

> **You:** "So here's the thing about what we do — we buy houses as-is. That means we're taking on all the repairs, all the closing costs, no agent fees. The trade-off is speed and convenience, but it does mean our offers are going to be different from what you'd see on Zillow."

### Step 2: Use a Range

Never give one number early on. **Ranges feel collaborative, not final.**

> **You:** "Based on what you've told me about the property — the condition, the location, the work it needs — homes like yours in that area are typically going to be in that $120,000 to $140,000 range on a cash, as-is basis. Does that feel like it's in the ballpark of what you were thinking?"

### Step 3: Gauge the Reaction

| Their Response | What It Means | Your Next Move |
|---|---|---|
| "Yeah, that's about what I expected" | ✅ Aligned | Move toward appointment |
| "I was hoping for more like $180K" | Gap exists but workable | Ask what drives that number, reframe |
| "That's way too low" | Big gap | Ask what they expected, reframe around repairs |
| **Silence** | They're processing | **Let it sit. Don't fill the silence.** |

---

## 🗣️ Example Scripts

### Anchor After Motivation Extraction

> **You:** "So you've got the house sitting vacant, you're paying that mortgage every month, and you've got $40K+ in repairs just to get it market-ready. Here's what I can tell you — in situations like yours, we typically see as-is offers somewhere in the $110K to $130K range. That lets you walk away clean — no repairs, no carrying costs, close in two weeks. How does that land with you?"

---

### Anchor When They Ask "What Can You Offer?"

> **You:** "I appreciate you asking. I don't want to throw out a number that doesn't mean anything — but based on what I know so far, and looking at what similar properties have sold for as-is in your area, we're probably looking at somewhere in the $90K to $110K range. That's not a final number — our acquisitions manager Kyle would need to walk through it — but I want to make sure we're in the same universe before we set that up. What do you think?"

---

### Anchor Using Repair Costs

> **You:** "So the roof alone is probably $8K–$12K, the HVAC is another $6K–$8K, and then you've got the kitchen and floors on top of that. We're looking at $30K–$40K in repairs minimum. When you back that out from what the house would sell for fixed up, that's how we get to our number. It's not about lowballing — it's math."

---

## 💡 The "Don't Anchor" Scenario

Sometimes anchoring early will **hurt** you. If the seller's expectations are actually **lower** than what you'd offer (rare but it happens), anchoring higher costs you money.

**The safe play** — when you're not sure, ask them first:

> **You:** "Before I run any numbers — do you have a price in mind that would make this work for you?"

If their number is lower than expected → great. If it's high → now you know the gap and can reframe.

---

## 🚨 Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| **Anchoring without context** | Explain WHY cash offers differ from retail first |
| **Anchoring too early** | Build rapport and extract motivation before any numbers |
| **Using one exact number** | "$130K" feels rigid. "$125K–$140K range" feels collaborative. |
| **Apologizing for your numbers** | Never say "I know this is low, but…" Present with confidence. |
| **Not gauging reaction** | Stop talking after you anchor. Their response tells you everything. |

---

## 📋 Quick Reference

1. **Anchor AFTER motivation, not before** — context and rapport first
2. **Use ranges, not single numbers** — feels collaborative, not final
3. **Explain the "why"** — repairs, as-is, no fees, speed. The number needs context.
4. **Let them react** — don't fill silence after you anchor. Their response tells you everything.
5. **Never apologize for your offer** — present with confidence. It's a fair trade for what we provide.
